Infrastructure investments may include utilities, transportation systems, digital infrastructure, and renewable power.           <p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    In our view, investors should move beyond siloed views of asset classes toward a broader understanding of how the built environment functions as a whole. Industrial assets have been supporting retail logistics and last-mile delivery. Airports have increasingly become consumer-oriented retail destinations. Residential buildings have incorporated hospitality and workplace functionality. Infrastructure and real estate have functioned less as separate categories and more as interconnected systems.
</p>
<p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    Historically, both infrastructure and real estate have delivered attractive combinations of return, income, and lower volatility relative to many public market asset classes, though with different risk and return profiles across the two sectors (see <strong class="intro--strong">Figure 5</strong>).
</p>
<p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    <strong>This convergence may create new opportunities for investors able to think holistically about real assets.</strong>
</p>

          <h2 class="intro intro--strong mar-2-5-rem">
    Better Together
</h2>
<p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    Infrastructure and real estate often deliver similar benefits, but in different proportions.
</p>
<p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    Core infrastructure has historically generated stable, income-oriented returns supported by regulated or contractual cash flows. Inflation protection is often embedded directly into pricing structures or concession agreements. Regulated utilities, for example, may benefit from contractual inflation linkage, regulated pricing structures, or rising replacement costs that allow revenues to increase alongside inflation.
</p>
<p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    Infrastructure may also include development-oriented strategies that offer less immediate income and higher risk profiles, especially in sectors tied to digital infrastructure or energy transition development.
</p>
<p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    Real estate could also provide attractive income generation, particularly in core and value-add strategies, but appreciation tends to play a larger role in total return. Real estate may benefit more directly from improving fundamentals, rental growth, and cyclical recoveries—notably after periods of market repricing, when supply constraints have begun to support occupancy and rent growth again.
</p>
<p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    The relationship could become even more compelling when the two sectors are combined.
</p>
<p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    Historically, adding real estate exposure to an infrastructure allocation improved return potential while only modestly increasing volatility (see <strong class="intro--strong">Figure 6</strong>). <strong>In Hines Research’s analysis, allocations combining infrastructure with up to 50% private real estate produced stronger risk-adjusted outcomes than infrastructure-only exposure</strong> while maintaining lower volatility than more growth-oriented private market strategies.
</p>
<p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    Beyond that level, however, the incremental return benefit historically appeared less compelling relative to the additional risk assumed.
</p>

Real estate, meanwhile, appears to be early in its recovery cycle following the reset that began in 2022 (see <strong class="intro--strong">Figure 7</strong>). <strong>If historical recovery dynamics hold, real estate investors could experience a period of above-trend “catch-up” returns as markets normalize over the next several years, though we expect this will vary by sector and geographic region.</strong>
</p>
<p class="mar-2-5-rem">
    Rather than replacing real estate exposure, rising infrastructure allocations may strengthen the case for pairing the two sectors together within a broader real assets strategy.

          <section class="box-section"><h2 class="intro intro--strong mar-zero full-width">Data Centers and Powered Land: A Case Study in Convergence</h2><p class="mar-2-5-rem">We believe that few sectors better illustrate the convergence of infrastructure and real estate than data centers.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">The explosive growth of AI, cloud computing, and digital connectivity has accelerated demand for data center capacity globally. But increasingly, the opportunity could extend beyond the buildings themselves.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">Power availability, land entitlement, and infrastructure readiness have become critical bottlenecks in the development pipeline. The scarce resources have often not been capital but execution—securing land, procuring power, navigating entitlement processes, and delivering projects in supply-constrained markets.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem"><i>This dynamic could create opportunities across both infrastructure and real estate strategies.</i></p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">Infrastructure investors may pursue long-duration digital infrastructure exposure through operational facilities and vertically integrated development strategies. Those investments may offer attractive return potential, but they also carry construction, leasing, and operational risk.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">Real estate managers, meanwhile, may approach the opportunity differently by focusing on powered land development and horizontal infrastructure. In many cases, managers may monetize sites before vertical construction begins, reducing exposure to facility construction and leasing risk while still seeking to benefit from growing demand for digital infrastructure capacity.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">In that sense, powered land may represent a differentiated risk-adjusted approach to the data center opportunity.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem"><strong>Hines Research estimates that approximately </strong><a href="https://www.hines.com/powered-land" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"><strong>40,000 acres of powered land</strong></a><strong> may be required globally over the next five years to support projected data center demand.</strong> That includes just the ground under the data centers themselves, and we expect significant further demand for space for ancillary office, logistics, and industrial development surrounding those data centers.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">The opportunity may be particularly compelling in parts of Europe in the near to medium term, though the U.S. and Asia also continue to present attractive development opportunities.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">The broader implication has been that some of the most compelling opportunities across real assets may increasingly emerge where infrastructure and real estate overlap.</p></section>

  <div class="l-content-grid__col">
            <article class="text">
          <h2 class="intro intro--strong mar-2-5-rem">Convergence Creates Opportunity</h2><p class="mar-2-5-rem full-width">Investing in real assets has become more capital-intensive, more operationally complex, and less defined by traditional asset class boundaries.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">Infrastructure continues to attract institutional capital because of its resilience, inflation sensitivity, and durable cash flows. Real estate, meanwhile, appears positioned to benefit from improving fundamentals, constrained supply, and a recovery already underway across several sectors. These complementary characteristics may prove even more valuable for investors concerned about the future of market growth and stability.&nbsp;</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">But the more important point may be convergence. Digital infrastructure, logistics networks, powered land, social infrastructure, and mixed-use development have increasingly linked infrastructure and real estate into a more integrated investment universe.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">That shift may create a broader opportunity set for investors able to underwrite both sectors together. In our view, the most compelling opportunities in real assets are likely to emerge where infrastructure and real estate overlap—and capitalizing on them will require fluency in both.</p><p class="mar-2-5-rem">Infrastructure and real estate may prove more powerful together than apart. We believe investors should evaluate both asset classes together within a broader real-assets framework.</p>
